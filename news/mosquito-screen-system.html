Mosquito Screen System – Integrated Protection for Your Entire Home

Protecting your home from insects requires more than just a single screen. A mosquito screen system integrates multiple screens across all openings – windows, doors, patios, and even porches – to create a complete barrier against flying pests. MBORMC designs custom mosquito screen systems tailored to your home’s architecture, window types, and insect pressure. In this guide, we’ll explain the components of a comprehensive screen system, how to plan your coverage, and the benefits of a unified approach to insect protection.

What Is a Mosquito Screen System?

A mosquito screen system is a coordinated set of insect screens installed on every opening of a building. It typically includes:

  • Window screens: Fixed, retractable, or pleated screens for all windows.
  • Door screens: Retractable, folding, sliding, or magnetic screens for all doors (entry, patio, French, sliding).
  • Porch or patio enclosures: Large folding or sliding screens to enclose outdoor living areas.
  • Ventilation screens: For attic fans, bathroom vents, or other openings.

The goal is to allow fresh air to flow freely while preventing any insect from entering. A complete system is especially important in areas with high mosquito‑borne disease risk or severe fly infestations.

Benefits of a Complete Screen System

  • Uniform protection: No weak points – insects can’t bypass unscreened openings.
  • Consistent aesthetics: All screens can be matched in frame colour and mesh colour for a cohesive look.
  • Simplified maintenance: Same cleaning procedures and replacement parts across the home.
  • Energy savings: You can keep windows and doors open for ventilation without insect concerns, reducing air conditioning usage.
  • Health protection: Prevents mosquito‑borne diseases (dengue, malaria, Zika) and flies bacteria contamination.

Planning Your Mosquito Screen System

MBORMC offers a free consultation to help you design your system. Steps include:

  1. Assess your property: Count all windows, doors, and other openings.
  2. Measure each opening: We provide a measurement guide or can arrange a video call to assist.
  3. Choose screen types per opening: For example, fixed frames for windows you rarely close, retractable for patio doors, magnetic for renters’ windows.
  4. Select mesh material and density: Standard PET 18×16 for most areas; upgrade to 20×20 or stainless steel for high‑risk zones or pets.
  5. Select frame colours: Match your window/door frames (white, grey, black, bronze, custom RAL).
  6. Order all screens together: We’ll manufacture and ship as a single package, saving on shipping costs.

System Components by Opening Type

  • Sliding windows: Sliding screens (rigid or pleated) or side‑mount retractable.
  • Casement windows: Fixed interior frame screens or top‑mount retractable.
  • Double‑hung windows: Fixed frame screens or Z‑frame self‑storing screens.
  • Single hinged doors: Retractable roll‑up screens (most popular) or magnetic curtains.
  • Sliding patio doors: Sliding screen doors (rigid frame) or side‑mount retractable.
  • French doors: Bi‑parting folding (pleated) screens that meet in the middle.
  • Large picture windows: Pleated folding screens that stack at one side, or multiple fixed frames for divided windows.
  • Porch or balcony enclosures: Extra‑wide folding screens (up to 4m per unit) or sliding screen walls.

Mesh Consistency Across the System

For a unified system, we recommend using the same mesh density and material throughout the home. However, you may choose to upgrade high‑risk areas (e.g., ground‑floor windows, kitchen doors) to stainless steel while using PET elsewhere. All meshes are available in grey, dark grey, or black for consistent appearance.

DIY vs. Professional Installation

Most MBORMC screens are designed for easy DIY installation. For large systems with dozens of screens, DIY is still possible if you have basic tools and patience. We provide detailed instructions for each screen type. Alternatively, we can recommend local installers in your area (fee may apply). For new construction or renovations, you can have your contractor install the screens during framing.
