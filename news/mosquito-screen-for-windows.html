Mosquito Screen for Windows – Complete Protection for Every Window in Your Home

Windows are the primary entry points for mosquitoes, flies, and other flying insects. A mosquito screen for windows allows you to keep your windows open for fresh air and natural light without inviting pests into your home. MBORMC manufactures custom screens for every window type – casement, sliding, double‑hung, awning, tilt‑and‑turn, and even arched or circular windows. In this comprehensive guide, we’ll cover the different screen styles, mesh materials, measurement tips, and installation methods to help you choose the perfect solution for your home.

Why Every Window Needs a Mosquito Screen

Open windows are essential for ventilation, especially during warm weather. But without a screen, they are an open invitation to insects. Mosquitoes can transmit diseases like dengue, malaria, and Zika; flies contaminate food; wasps can sting. A window screen provides a physical barrier that stops insects while letting in air and light. Unlike chemical sprays or electronic devices, screens work 24/7 without any ongoing cost. They also keep out dust, pollen, and small debris, improving indoor air quality.

Types of Window Screens from MBORMC

We offer several screen styles to match different window configurations:

  • Fixed Frame Screens – A rigid screen mounted directly to the window frame with screws or clips. Most common for windows that are opened frequently. Provides the tightest seal and is very durable.
  • Retractable Roll‑Up Screens – The mesh rolls into a slim cassette mounted at the top or side of the window. When not in use, the screen is completely hidden. Ideal for windows you don’t always need screened.
  • Sliding Screens – For horizontal sliding windows, a sliding screen moves on a track. It can be a rigid frame that slides or a flexible pleated screen.
  • Pleated (Folding) Screens – The mesh folds like an accordion and stacks at one side. Great for wide windows or where a cassette cannot be installed.
  • Magnetic Screens – Attaches to the window frame via magnetic strips. No drilling, no tools – perfect for renters or temporary use.
  • Z‑Frame (Self‑Storing) Screens – A fixed frame that is slightly larger than the window opening and held in place by spring clips – easy to remove for cleaning.

Compatibility with Different Window Types

  • Casement windows (crank out): A fixed frame screen on the interior side works best. Ensure the screen does not interfere with the crank mechanism.
  • Sliding windows (horizontal slider): Use a rigid sliding screen that rides on a track, or a pleated screen that stacks at one side.
  • Double‑hung / sash windows: A fixed frame screen that covers the entire window or separate screens for each sash. Spring‑loaded Z‑frames work well for easy removal.
  • Awning windows (hinged at top): A fixed frame screen on the inside is typical. Ensure the screen is low‑profile so it doesn’t block the opening arc.
  • Tilt‑and‑turn windows: A fixed frame screen mounted on the interior side works well. The screen should be positioned to allow the window to tilt inward without hitting.
  • Arched or round windows: MBORMC can manufacture custom‑shaped frames to match any non‑rectangular window. Provide a template or detailed drawing.

Mesh Material: PET vs. 304 Stainless Steel

PET fiber mesh (18×16 density): Lightweight, affordable, and offers excellent visibility (85% openness). UV‑resistant and rust‑proof. Lasts 5–10 years. Ideal for most residential windows.

304 stainless steel mesh (18×16 or 20×20): Extremely strong, pet‑proof (resists scratching), fire‑resistant, and lasts 10–20 years. Slightly less transparent (75% openness) but much more durable. Recommended for ground‑floor windows or homes with pets.

Both meshes are available in grey, dark grey, or black. Darker mesh reduces glare and improves outward visibility.

How to Measure for a Window Screen

  • For windows that open inward: measure the inside opening width and height at three points (top, middle, bottom; left, center, right). Use the smallest measurement.
  • For windows that open outward: measure the outside frame dimensions.
  • Subtract 2‑3mm from each dimension to allow for easy installation (unless you order a Z‑frame, which is made slightly larger and held by clips).
  • For arched windows: measure the width at the widest point and the height from the top of the arch to the bottom. Provide a photo with a ruler for reference.

DIY Installation – Simple Steps

Installing a window mosquito screen is a straightforward DIY project:

  • Fixed frame screens: Clean the window frame, then place the screen into the opening. Secure with screws (pre‑drill holes in the frame) or use spring clips (no drilling).
  • Retractable screens: Mount the cassette above the window using screws (wall anchors provided). Attach the side guides, then pull the screen down and latch it.
  • Sliding screens: Install the top and bottom tracks, then slide the screen panel into place.
  • Magnetic screens: Clean the window frame, apply the self‑adhesive magnetic tape, then press the screen onto the tape.

All hardware is included. Basic tools like a drill and screwdriver may be needed for some types. Installation time: 10–30 minutes per window.

Frequently Asked Questions

Q: Can I install a window screen on a window that opens outward?
A: Yes. For outward‑opening windows, we recommend an interior‑mounted fixed frame screen or a retractable screen that doesn’t interfere with the opening mechanism.

Q: Will a window screen block my view?
A: Our high‑transparency PET mesh has over 85% openness. You’ll barely notice it from inside. Dark mesh also reduces glare.

Q: How do I clean my window screen?
A: Vacuum with a brush attachment, or wipe with a damp cloth. Removable screens can be rinsed with mild soap and water.

Q: Do you offer screens for very large picture windows?
A: Yes. For fixed picture windows that never open, you may not need a screen. But if you want to open them, we can provide extra‑wide retractable or folding screens.
